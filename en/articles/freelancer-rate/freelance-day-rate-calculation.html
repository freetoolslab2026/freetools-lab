How to calculate your freelance day rate in 2026

A practical guide to pricing freelance work properly: income target, tax buffer, expenses, billable days, market positioning and rate negotiation.

Summary: a sustainable freelance day rate is not your desired salary divided by working days. It must cover tax, business expenses, unpaid time, holidays, sickness, admin, sales, professional risk and market positioning.

The core freelance day rate formula

The simplest useful freelance pricing formula is: required gross revenue equals target net income plus business expenses, divided by one minus your tax buffer. Once you know required gross revenue, divide it by realistic billable days. That gives your required day rate. It sounds basic, but it prevents the most common pricing mistake: treating freelancing like salaried employment.

If you want to keep £70,000 per year, spend £8,000 on business costs and set aside 30% for tax and social contributions, your required gross revenue is not £78,000. It is £78,000 divided by 0.70, which is about £111,429. If you can bill 220 days, the required day rate is roughly £506. If you can bill only 170 days, the required day rate becomes about £655. The same income target can therefore require very different rates depending on billable capacity.

This is why freelancers often undercharge when they start. They compare their desired income with a salary, but forget that a salary comes with employer-paid benefits, paid holidays, predictable payroll, sick pay in some countries, equipment, admin support, pension contributions or health coverage. A freelancer has to fund many of these directly or absorb the risk.

The formula does not tell you whether the market will accept your price. It tells you the minimum rate that makes your business model financially coherent. The next step is to compare that rate with market benchmarks and client value. If your required rate is far above market for your sector and seniority, the answer is not always “charge less”. It may be to specialise, target higher-value clients, reduce expenses, improve utilisation or change your service offer.

Why billable days are the hidden lever

Billable days are the number of days you can actually invoice clients. They are not the same as working days. A year may contain around 250 working days before holidays, depending on country and calendar. But freelancers rarely bill every working day. Time disappears into sales calls, proposals, marketing, bookkeeping, training, portfolio work, client gaps, illness, holidays and unpaid admin.

For a full-time freelancer, 220 billable days is an optimistic but possible assumption if demand is strong and admin is efficient. Many independent professionals are closer to 160-200 billable days. Consultants with long contracts may exceed 220. Creative freelancers with project gaps may be below 150. The correct number depends on your business model.

Small changes matter. Suppose your required annual gross is $120,000. At 220 billable days, your day rate is $545. At 180 billable days, it is $667. At 150 billable days, it is $800. The difference is not greed; it is utilisation. If you sell short projects with frequent gaps, your rate must be higher than someone on a long retainer with predictable workload.

New freelancers should be conservative. It is better to assume fewer billable days and be pleasantly surprised than to build a business plan on full utilisation. If your pipeline becomes stable, you can reduce the risk buffer later or use the extra margin for savings, pension, tax reserves and investment in skills.

Business expenses and tax buffer

Business expenses vary widely by profession. A developer may need laptop, software, cloud tools, professional insurance, accounting support and training. A photographer may need cameras, lenses, editing software, storage, travel and insurance. A consultant may need research tools, events, legal review, proposal software and subcontractors. These costs reduce what you actually keep.

Do not hide expenses inside your personal income target. Keep them separate. This improves clarity and makes price increases easier to justify. If software subscriptions rise by 20%, your required gross revenue changes. If you need to hire a bookkeeper, your day rate should reflect that.

The tax buffer is not a precise tax calculation. It is a planning reserve. In some countries, 25-30% may be enough for moderate incomes. In others, self-employment taxes, social contributions, VAT/GST handling, health insurance and pension contributions may require a higher buffer. High earners may need 40% or more. The safest approach is to ask an accountant for your specific country and structure, then use the calculator with that percentage.

A good habit is to separate tax money immediately. Move the tax buffer into a separate account as soon as invoices are paid. Freelancers get into trouble when they treat gross receipts as disposable income. Revenue is not profit, and profit is not personal spending money until tax and costs are covered.

How to use market benchmarks without copying them blindly

Benchmarks are useful, but they are not laws. Public freelance rate reports show averages or medians across many disciplines, experience levels and contract types. For example, YunoJuno’s 2025 report states an average UK freelance day rate of £390 across its dataset. IT contractor data can show much higher medians for specific technology roles. Arc.dev reports broad hourly ranges for vetted developers. These numbers are all true in context, but they are not interchangeable.

A senior software engineer on a bank contract in London is not comparable to a junior copywriter doing small business web copy, even if both call themselves freelancers. A UX designer with conversion case studies can charge differently from a designer selling isolated screens. A consultant who owns a business outcome can price differently from someone selling capacity.

Use benchmarks to answer three questions. First, is your calculated rate below the bottom quartile, around median or above the upper quartile? Second, does your positioning justify that place? Third, what would you need to prove to move up? If you want p75 rates, you need p75-level evidence: outcomes, specialist skill, speed, reliability, niche demand or access to scarce expertise.

If your required rate is above market, do not immediately panic. Check billable days, expenses and tax buffer. Then check your offer. Can you sell packages instead of time? Can you target clients for whom the work has higher financial value? Can you specialise in a regulated or urgent niche? Price is partly arithmetic and partly positioning.

Worked examples

Example one: a UK senior software developer wants £80,000 net, has £10,000 expenses, uses a 30% tax buffer and expects 210 billable days. Required gross is £90,000 divided by 0.70, or £128,571. Divided by 210 days, the required rate is about £612 per day. That is close to current senior developer contractor benchmarks in the UK, so the target may be realistic if the freelancer has strong demand and good positioning.

Example two: a mid-level marketing freelancer in the United States wants $90,000 net, has $12,000 expenses, uses a 32% tax buffer and expects 180 billable days. Required gross is $102,000 divided by 0.68, or $150,000. Divided by 180 days, the required rate is $833. If local market benchmarks are lower, the freelancer needs to sell higher-value outcomes, not generic execution.

Example three: a Spanish freelance designer wants €45,000 net, has €5,000 expenses, uses a 30% buffer and expects 170 billable days. Required gross is €50,000 divided by 0.70, or €71,429. Divided by 170 days, the required day rate is €420. If the local market median is lower, the freelancer may need international clients, productised services or a narrower niche.

When to raise your rate

You should review your rate at least once a year. Inflation alone can erode your income. If you charged £500 three years ago and inflation averaged 3% per year, the equivalent today is about £546. If your skills, demand and case studies improved, the new rate may be higher. Not raising rates is effectively giving clients a discount every year.

Raise rates when your pipeline is full, when repeat clients book you easily, when projects create measurable value, when your niche becomes more specialised or when your costs rise. Do not wait until resentment builds. A small, regular increase is easier to communicate than a sudden large jump after years of underpricing.

For existing clients, give notice and frame the increase professionally. Explain the new rate, effective date and any change in scope or service level. Some clients will leave. That is not always bad. If low-paying clients block capacity for better work, keeping them can be more expensive than losing them.

Hourly, daily or project pricing?

Hourly pricing is simple and transparent for small tasks, support and advisory calls. It is less effective for outcomes because it rewards time spent, not value delivered. Daily pricing works well for contracting, workshops and retained capacity. Project pricing can be best when scope is clear and value is high, but it requires strong boundaries.

Many freelancers use all three. Hourly for small support, day rate for consulting or implementation, project price for defined deliverables and retainers for ongoing access. The day rate remains useful because it anchors your economics. Even when you quote a fixed project, you should know how many internal days it implies and whether the effective day rate is acceptable.

How to build a rate floor and a premium rate

Every freelancer should know two numbers: the rate floor and the premium rate. The rate floor is the lowest rate that keeps the business sustainable after tax, expenses and unpaid time. You should not go below it unless there is a deliberate strategic reason, such as entering a new niche with a short trial project. The premium rate is the rate you quote when the client has urgency, high value, complex risk or a strong fit for your specialist expertise.

The mistake is to use one flat rate for every client. A small charity, a venture-backed SaaS company and a regulated financial institution do not value the same work in the same way. If the value, risk and urgency are different, the commercial context is different. You can keep your internal rate floor stable while packaging and quoting work differently depending on the situation.

For example, your rate floor may be £500 per day. For a straightforward implementation with low risk, you may quote £600. For an urgent rescue project where delay costs the client revenue, £850 may be reasonable. For a strategy workshop that saves months of wasted work, a fixed package may be better than a day rate. The day rate is the foundation, not the ceiling.

How to avoid hidden unpaid work

Freelancers often undercharge because they forget invisible labour. A client meeting is visible. Writing the proposal, reviewing notes, following up, preparing the invoice, chasing payment, researching the client, updating the portfolio and maintaining tools are often invisible. If your rate only covers delivery hours, you slowly subsidise clients with unpaid admin.

Track a few projects honestly. Count sales calls, preparation, delivery, revisions, communication, invoicing and follow-up. Then calculate the effective day rate. A project quoted at £2,000 may look profitable until you realise it took six full days across three weeks. The effective rate is then £333 per day, not the £500 you thought you were charging.

This is why project pricing and retainers need boundaries. Define what is included, how many revision rounds are covered, how communication works, what counts as out of scope and when extra work is billed. Better boundaries do not make you less helpful. They make the business sustainable.

How to test a new rate

You do not need to raise every client at once. Start with new leads. Quote the new rate to the next three qualified prospects and observe the response. If all accept immediately, you were probably undercharging. If some negotiate but still proceed, the rate may be right. If every qualified lead rejects and gives similar feedback, refine positioning, proof or target market.

Do not test pricing on poor-fit leads. A client with no budget will reject any professional rate and teach you nothing. Test with clients who have a real problem, budget, urgency and respect for specialist work. Pricing feedback is only useful when the lead is qualified.

Keep notes. Track quoted rate, accepted rate, sector, lead source, project size and objections. Over time you will see which markets support higher rates and which channels produce low-budget work. This data is more useful than generic online advice.

FAQ

What is a good freelance day rate?

A good day rate covers your income target, tax, expenses and unpaid time, while staying credible for your sector, seniority and market.

How many billable days should I assume?

Many freelancers use 160-220. Use a lower number if you have short projects, seasonal work or heavy business development time.

Should I copy market median rates?

No. Use medians as positioning signals, then price based on value, specialisation, demand and your financial needs.

How often should I raise rates?

Review at least annually. Inflation, experience, demand and stronger case studies are all valid reasons to increase rates.
