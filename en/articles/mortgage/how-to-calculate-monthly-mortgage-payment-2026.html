How to Calculate Your Monthly Mortgage Payment in 2026

Quick answer
A monthly mortgage payment is not just the loan payment. A realistic estimate should include principal and interest, property taxes, homeowners insurance, mortgage insurance when required, and any HOA fees. The principal and interest amount comes from the loan amount, rate and term. Taxes, insurance and PMI depend on local data, loan type and lender rules.

When buyers ask, “How much will my mortgage be?”, they often mean the full monthly housing payment. That is the number that affects your budget, not just the principal and interest payment shown in a simple loan formula. A lender may quote principal and interest clearly, but your bank account usually feels the total payment: loan repayment, escrowed property taxes, homeowners insurance, possible mortgage insurance and, in some communities, HOA fees.

This distinction matters because two homes with the same price can have very different monthly costs. A $420,000 home in a high-tax county may cost more each month than a more expensive home in a lower-tax area. A buyer with a 10% down payment may pay private mortgage insurance, while a buyer with 20% down may avoid it on a conventional loan. A condo buyer may also face HOA dues that are not part of the loan but still affect affordability.

The basic mortgage payment formula

The core loan payment is the monthly principal and interest payment. This is the part determined by three inputs: loan amount, interest rate and loan term. If you borrow more, the payment rises. If the rate is higher, the payment rises. If the term is shorter, the monthly payment usually rises, but the total interest paid over the life of the loan usually falls.

Principal and interest formula
Monthly P&I = P × [r(1+r)n] ÷ [(1+r)n − 1]

Where P is the loan amount, r is the monthly interest rate, and n is the total number of monthly payments.

For example, if you buy a home for $400,000 and make an $80,000 down payment, your loan amount is $320,000. On a 30-year mortgage, that means 360 monthly payments. The interest rate is converted into a monthly rate before applying the formula. This is why a small change in the interest rate can make a large difference over time: the rate affects every month of a long repayment schedule.

However, principal and interest are only the first layer. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau explains the difference between principal-and-interest payments and total monthly payments by adding mortgage insurance when applicable and escrow items such as homeowners insurance and property tax. If the home is in a condo, co-op or homeowners association, additional fees may also apply separately.

What goes into the full monthly mortgage payment?

A practical mortgage estimate should be built component by component. Some parts are fixed by the loan. Others depend on the property, the state, the county, the insurer, the lender and the type of mortgage. Step 1: Start with the home price and down payment

The home price is not the same as the loan amount. The loan amount is the purchase price minus the down payment. If you buy a $450,000 home and put down $45,000, the loan amount is $405,000. If you put down $90,000, the loan amount is $360,000. That difference changes the principal and interest payment, the loan-to-value ratio and potentially the mortgage insurance requirement.

The down payment also affects risk. On a conventional loan, PMI is commonly required when the down payment is less than 20% of the purchase price. The CFPB describes PMI as insurance that protects the lender if the borrower stops making payments; it is not insurance that protects the borrower from foreclosure or missed payments.

This is why the same home price can produce different monthly payments for different buyers. A buyer with 5% down borrows more and may pay PMI. A buyer with 20% down borrows less and may avoid conventional PMI. The larger down payment may also improve loan options, though it can reduce cash reserves. Do not drain every dollar just to hit a down payment target if it leaves you unable to handle repairs, moving costs or emergencies.

Step 2: Add the interest rate and loan term

The interest rate and loan term drive the principal and interest payment. A 30-year mortgage spreads the loan over 360 months, which generally lowers the monthly payment compared with a 15-year mortgage. The trade-off is that interest has more time to accumulate. A 15-year mortgage usually has a higher monthly payment but a much lower total interest cost if the borrower can comfortably afford it.

Rate shopping matters. Even a small difference can affect both the monthly payment and the total interest paid. When comparing offers, look beyond the headline rate. Points, lender credits, fees and APR can change the real cost. A lower interest rate with high upfront points may or may not be better depending on how long you keep the loan.

The calculator can show the monthly payment and amortization pattern, but it is not a lender quote. A lender quote can include credit score adjustments, points, escrow requirements, mortgage insurance pricing, lock timing and closing cost details. Use the calculator to understand the structure, then compare official Loan Estimates from lenders before committing.

Step 3: Estimate property taxes

Property taxes are one of the biggest reasons mortgage payments vary by location. They are usually set locally, not nationally. State averages can be useful for a first estimate, but they are not enough for a final buying decision. County, city, school district, exemptions and reassessment rules can all change the actual tax bill.

If you are still browsing homes, a state or county average may be acceptable for early planning. Once you have a specific property, look up the current tax bill and check how reassessment works after purchase. In some areas, the current owner’s tax bill may be lower than what a new buyer will pay after the sale price resets the assessed value.

Many borrowers pay property taxes through escrow. In that case, the lender collects a monthly amount as part of the mortgage payment and later pays the tax bill when due. Escrow can make budgeting easier, but the amount can change if taxes rise or if the escrow account needs adjustment.

Step 4: Estimate homeowners insurance

Homeowners insurance is another cost that can shift substantially by state, region and property risk. Homes exposed to hurricanes, wildfires, hail, flooding or other hazards may face higher premiums or additional coverage needs. The calculator’s state defaults are only a starting point. A real insurance quote is much better.

Be careful with online affordability estimates that use a generic insurance number. A $100 monthly assumption may be too low in some markets. In high-risk areas, insurance can be a major part of the monthly housing cost. Also remember that standard homeowners insurance does not necessarily cover every risk. Flood insurance, windstorm coverage or other policies may be separate depending on the property.

For a strong estimate, request an insurance quote before finalizing your budget. Enter that annual premium into the calculator instead of relying on a default. This gives a more realistic monthly payment and reduces the chance of being surprised after the offer is accepted.

Step 5: Include PMI when the down payment is below 20%

Private mortgage insurance is often misunderstood. PMI does not pay your mortgage if you lose income. It protects the lender if you default. For conventional loans, it is commonly required when the borrower puts down less than 20%. The cost depends on loan-to-value, credit score, loan type, term and lender pricing.

PMI can still be useful if it lets you buy a home without waiting years to save 20% down. The question is not simply “PMI bad, 20% down good.” The real question is whether the full monthly payment, including PMI, fits your budget and whether buying now is better than waiting. In fast-moving markets, waiting to avoid PMI may or may not save money. In expensive markets, PMI may be the cost of entering earlier.

The calculator estimates PMI when the down payment is below 20%, but lender-level accuracy requires the actual PMI rate from your lender. If your Loan Estimate shows a specific monthly mortgage insurance amount, use that figure as the source of truth.

Step 6: Do not ignore HOA fees

HOA fees can make a home much more expensive than the loan payment suggests. Condos, townhomes and some planned communities may charge monthly dues for shared maintenance, amenities, insurance, reserves or management. These fees are usually separate from principal and interest, but they affect your monthly cash flow.

HOA dues can also rise. Before buying, review the association budget, reserve study if available, recent fee increases and any special assessments. A low monthly HOA fee is not automatically good if the association is underfunded. A higher fee is not automatically bad if it covers major maintenance that you would otherwise pay directly.

For mortgage planning, include HOA dues in your housing budget even if they are not paid to the lender. Your lender may also consider HOA dues when calculating debt-to-income ratio.

How amortization changes the payment over time

On a fixed-rate mortgage, the principal and interest payment usually stays the same, but the internal split changes every month. Early payments are interest-heavy because the loan balance is still high. Later payments send more money toward principal because the balance has declined. This is called amortization.

Amortization is important because the monthly payment does not tell the whole story. In the first years of a 30-year mortgage, you may build equity slowly through principal repayment. Equity can still grow if the home value rises, but that is not guaranteed. If you plan to move in only a few years, understanding early interest cost matters.

Extra principal payments can shorten the loan and reduce total interest, but they are not always the best use of cash. Compare extra payments with emergency savings, retirement contributions, higher-interest debt and planned repairs. A calculator can show the mortgage impact, but the decision depends on your wider financial situation.

Conforming loan limits in 2026

For 2026, the baseline conforming loan limit for a one-unit property in the contiguous United States, District of Columbia and Puerto Rico is $832,750 according to published conforming loan limit data. Higher limits apply in certain high-cost areas and in Alaska, Hawaii, Guam and the U.S. Virgin Islands.

This does not mean you cannot borrow more than the baseline. It means loans above the applicable conforming limit may be treated differently, often as jumbo loans, with different underwriting and pricing. If your loan amount is near the limit, check the applicable county limit and ask your lender how it affects your options.

Common mistakes when estimating a mortgage payment

The biggest mistake is using only principal and interest. That produces a number that looks attractive but may be far below the real monthly cost. The second mistake is using national averages for property tax and insurance when local values are available. The third mistake is ignoring PMI until late in the process.

Another common mistake is comparing rent to mortgage principal and interest only. A homeowner also pays repairs, maintenance, insurance, taxes and sometimes HOA assessments. Some of those costs are monthly; others arrive irregularly. A responsible budget leaves room for both.

Finally, buyers sometimes rely too much on preapproval. A lender may approve a payment that feels uncomfortable for your actual life. Approval is not the same as affordability. Your personal budget should include savings goals, transportation, healthcare, childcare, taxes, insurance and a cushion for unexpected costs.

Mortgage payment checklist

  • Use the purchase price, not just the listing price if concessions or credits change the deal.
  • Subtract the down payment to get the loan amount.
  • Use the actual interest rate and loan term from your lender quote.
  • Enter county-level property tax whenever possible.
  • Replace insurance defaults with a real homeowners insurance quote.
  • Add PMI if the down payment is below 20% on a conventional loan.
  • Add HOA dues if the home belongs to an association.
  • Review the amortization schedule if you care about total interest and equity growth.

Bottom line

A mortgage payment is a stack of costs, not a single number. Principal and interest are the mathematical core, but the realistic monthly housing payment also includes taxes, insurance, mortgage insurance when required and HOA dues when applicable. The safest approach is to start with a calculator, then replace every estimate with real property-specific and lender-specific data as you move closer to buying.

Use a planning estimate early, but do not confuse it with a final lender quote. When you have a property address, lender Loan Estimate, insurance quote and local tax information, your monthly payment estimate becomes much more reliable.

Mortgage payment FAQ

P&I means principal and interest. PITI means principal, interest, taxes and insurance. PITI is closer to the real housing payment, but PMI and HOA fees may still need to be added.
On many conventional loans, PMI is commonly required when the down payment is below 20%. Actual requirements depend on loan type, lender rules, credit profile and loan-to-value.
Often they are collected through escrow, but this depends on the lender and loan arrangement. Even if paid separately, property taxes should be included in your housing budget.
A lender quote may include exact escrow rules, points, credits, PMI pricing, fees, credit adjustments and local tax data. A calculator is a planning estimate unless you enter exact quote-level data.
A 15-year mortgage usually costs less in total interest but has a higher monthly payment. A 30-year mortgage usually lowers the monthly payment but increases total interest over time.
