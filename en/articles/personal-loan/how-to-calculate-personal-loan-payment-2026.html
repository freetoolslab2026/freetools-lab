How to Calculate a Personal Loan Payment in 2026

Learn how loan amount, APR, term, origination fees and extra payments affect the monthly payment and total cost of a fixed-rate personal loan.

Short answer
A personal loan payment is calculated from the amount financed, APR and loan term. A longer term lowers the monthly payment but usually increases total interest. Origination fees can make the loan more expensive or reduce the cash you actually receive. To compare offers correctly, look at monthly payment, APR, total interest, fees and total cost together.

A personal loan looks simple because it usually has one fixed monthly payment. That simplicity is useful, but it can also hide the real cost. Two offers can show the same monthly payment and still have different costs because one loan has a longer term, a higher origination fee, a different APR, or a fee deducted before the money reaches your bank account.

This guide explains how to calculate a personal loan payment in 2026 without turning the process into a black box. The goal is not to push you toward borrowing. The goal is to help you understand what the payment actually means before you sign anything.

What you need before calculating the payment

To calculate a personal loan payment, you need four main inputs: the loan amount, the APR or interest rate, the repayment term and any lender fees. If the lender gives you an official APR, that is usually the better comparison number because APR reflects the interest rate plus certain fees. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau explains APR as a measure that includes the interest rate and additional loan fees, while both are shown as percentages.

The loan term is usually stated in months, such as 24, 36, 48, 60 or 84 months. The longer the term, the more months you have to repay the balance. That normally lowers the monthly payment, but the loan has more time to accumulate interest.

Fees matter because personal installment loans can include charges such as origination fees, documentation fees, optional credit insurance or late fees. The CFPB lists origination fees and documentation fees among common personal installment loan fees.

The basic personal loan payment formula

Most personal loans are fixed-rate installment loans. That means the loan is repaid through equal scheduled payments, and each payment contains some interest and some principal. At the start, more of the payment goes to interest. Near the end, more goes to principal.

Monthly payment formula
Monthly payment = loan balance × monthly rate ÷ [1 − (1 + monthly rate)−number of months]

Monthly rate = annual APR ÷ 12. If the APR is 12%, the monthly rate is 1%.

You do not need to memorize the formula to make a good decision. What matters is understanding what each variable does. The loan balance is the amount being amortized. The APR controls how fast interest accrues. The term controls how many payments spread the cost over time.

Example: $15,000 personal loan over 60 months

Suppose you borrow $15,000 for 60 months at 11.40% APR. The Federal Reserve’s G.19 data, published through FRED, shows the finance rate on 24-month personal loans at commercial banks was 11.40% in February 2026. That is not a universal market quote, but it is a useful public benchmark for understanding the environment.

InputExample valueWhy it matters
Loan amount$15,000The principal borrowed before any lender fee treatment.
APR11.40%The annualized cost used to calculate interest.
Term60 monthsSpreads repayment across five years.
Origination fee3%May be financed, deducted from proceeds or paid upfront.

With no fee and no extra payment, the scheduled monthly payment would be roughly $329. The total paid over 60 months would be about $19,740, which means total interest would be about $4,740. The exact result depends on rounding and payment timing, but the point is clear: the monthly payment does not tell you the full cost by itself.

APR vs interest rate

The interest rate is the charge for borrowing money. APR is broader because it can include lender fees. That makes APR a better comparison tool when two loans have different fee structures. A loan with a lower interest rate but a high origination fee may not be cheaper than a loan with a slightly higher rate and no fee.

This distinction matters because lenders often advertise low monthly payments or headline rates. Those numbers are not useless, but they are incomplete. You should compare:

  • APR, not only the interest rate.
  • Total interest over the full term.
  • Origination fee and how it is charged.
  • Cash actually received after deducted fees.
  • Any prepayment penalty or late fee rules.

If a lender gives you a Truth in Lending disclosure or loan agreement, use those official numbers over any rough online estimate. A calculator is for planning; the signed documents control the actual loan.

How origination fees change the real cost

An origination fee is a charge for setting up or processing the loan. Lenders may describe it as a percentage of the loan amount. For example, a 3% origination fee on a $15,000 loan is $450. What happens next depends on the lender’s fee treatment.

Fee treatmentWhat happensWhy it matters
Financed into balanceThe fee is added to the loan balance.You pay interest on a larger balance.
Deducted from proceedsThe lender subtracts the fee before sending funds.You may receive less cash than the headline loan amount.
Paid upfrontYou pay the fee separately at closing/funding.The monthly payment may not show that upfront cost.

Imagine you need exactly $15,000 in cash. If the lender deducts a $450 fee from a $15,000 loan, you may receive only $14,550. To actually receive $15,000, you might need to borrow more. That is why “cash received” is a critical number, especially for debt consolidation, medical bills, home repairs or major purchases.

Short term vs long term

The loan term is one of the biggest levers in the calculation. A longer term lowers the monthly payment because the balance is spread across more months. But that lower payment is not free. You usually pay interest for longer.

For the same loan amount and APR, a 36-month loan will usually have a higher monthly payment than a 60-month loan, but the 36-month loan can cost much less in total interest. A 72-month or 84-month loan may feel easier each month, yet it can keep the debt around longer and increase the total cost.

Practical rule
Choose the shortest term you can comfortably afford without creating a cash-flow problem. Do not choose a term only because the monthly payment looks smaller.

How extra payments affect payoff

Extra payments can reduce the total interest because they lower the remaining balance faster. If your lender applies the extra amount directly to principal, even a small extra payment can shorten the payoff timeline. The effect is strongest when the extra payment starts early.

However, you should check two things before relying on this strategy. First, confirm that the loan has no prepayment penalty. Second, confirm that extra payments are applied to principal rather than simply advancing the next due date. Lender rules vary, and the wrong payment handling can reduce the benefit.

How to compare two personal loan offers

Do not compare personal loans by monthly payment alone. A lower payment can be the result of a longer term, not a better loan. A better comparison includes the full cost and the cash you actually receive.

  1. Write down the loan amount and cash received.
  2. Compare APR, not only interest rate.
  3. Check the origination fee and fee treatment.
  4. Calculate total interest over the full term.
  5. Check total cost of borrowing.
  6. Review prepayment penalties, late fees and autopay conditions.
  7. Make sure the monthly payment fits your budget without relying on best-case assumptions.

A personal loan can be useful when it replaces more expensive debt, funds a necessary expense, or gives you predictable fixed payments. It can also be a bad idea if it stretches your budget, finances nonessential spending, or only moves debt around without changing the behavior that created the debt.

When the calculator result may differ from your lender

A personal loan calculator is exact only for the assumptions you enter. Your lender’s real offer may include payment timing rules, fee disclosures, autopay discounts, state-specific rules, credit-based pricing, late fees or optional insurance products. If the lender’s APR already includes certain fees, do not double-count the same fee again in your calculation.

Also be careful with advertised ranges. A lender may advertise a low APR, but your actual rate depends on credit score, income, existing debt, loan amount, term, lender underwriting and sometimes state availability. Treat advertisements as a starting point, not a guarantee.

Bottom line

To calculate a personal loan payment correctly, start with the amount financed, APR and term. Then add the details most people overlook: origination fees, fee treatment, cash received, total interest and payoff time. The monthly payment matters because it affects your budget, but the total cost matters because it tells you what the loan really costs.

The best personal loan calculation is not just “Can I afford the payment?” It is “Do I understand the total cost, the fees, the payoff timeline and the alternatives?” If the answer is no, slow down and compare before signing.
