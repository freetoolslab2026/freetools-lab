Am I on Track for Retirement? How to Estimate Your Retirement Income Gap

A practical guide to checking retirement readiness by comparing target spending with Social Security, projected savings, inflation and portfolio withdrawal income.

Short answer
You are broadly on track for retirement if your projected portfolio income plus Social Security can cover your target spending after inflation. The retirement income gap is the monthly difference between what you expect to need and what your projected income can provide. A positive gap means a potential shortfall; a negative gap means a projected surplus under your assumptions.

What does “on track for retirement” actually mean?

Being on track for retirement does not mean reaching one magic number. It means your expected retirement income is reasonably aligned with the spending you want to support. A person with a paid-off home, modest expenses and strong Social Security may need a much smaller portfolio than someone with rent, debt, high healthcare costs and limited guaranteed income.

The cleanest way to think about retirement readiness is not “Do I have enough saved?” but “Will my future income cover my future expenses?” That turns retirement planning into a gap calculation. You estimate your target spending, subtract income sources such as Social Security, then test whether your savings can produce the rest.

This method is more useful than age-based rules because it adjusts to real life. Two households may both have $500,000 saved at age 55. One may be behind because it needs $8,000 a month and plans to retire at 62. The other may be on track because it needs $3,500 a month and plans to work until 68. The balance matters, but the gap matters more.

The retirement income gap formula

The basic formula is simple:

StepCalculationWhat it tells you
1Target monthly spendingThe lifestyle you want to fund in retirement.
2Minus Social Security and other incomeThe amount not covered by outside income.
3Equals monthly income gapThe amount your savings portfolio must support.
4Gap × 12 ÷ withdrawal rateA rough portfolio target.
5Compare target with projected savingsShows whether the current path looks reasonable.

Suppose your target spending is $6,000 per month in retirement and your Social Security estimate is $2,400 per month. Your gap is $3,600 per month, or $43,200 per year. Using a 4% withdrawal assumption, that gap implies a portfolio target near $1,080,000 before taxes. At a 3.5% withdrawal assumption, the target rises to roughly $1,234,000.

The purpose of the formula is not to predict the exact future. It is to make the problem visible. If your projected portfolio is $1,200,000 and your target is $1,080,000, you may be broadly on track under those assumptions. If your projection is $600,000, the plan probably needs adjustment. The useful question is which lever changes the result most: saving more, retiring later, spending less, using a lower target, or improving assumptions carefully.

Start with spending, not salary

A common retirement shortcut says you may need a certain percentage of pre-retirement income. That can be a rough starting point, but it is weaker than a spending-based plan. Retirement is funded by expenses, not by your old salary. A household earning $120,000 and spending $65,000 is different from a household earning the same amount and spending $110,000.

Build a realistic monthly spending target by separating essentials and lifestyle spending. Essentials include housing, utilities, food, transportation, insurance, healthcare and taxes. Lifestyle spending includes travel, gifts, hobbies, restaurants and family support. Do not assume every expense disappears in retirement. Payroll taxes may fall, retirement contributions may stop, and commuting costs may shrink, but healthcare, travel, home repairs and insurance can rise.

Use today’s dollars first because they are easier to understand. If $5,000 per month feels realistic today, use that as a current-dollar target. Then either inflate the spending target to retirement age or use a real-return calculator that keeps everything in today’s dollars. What matters is consistency.

Inflation can turn a comfortable number into a shortfall

Inflation is one of the easiest planning mistakes to underestimate. If retirement is decades away, future spending will usually be much higher in nominal dollars than today’s spending. At 2.5% annual inflation, $5,000 per month today becomes more than $8,000 per month after about 20 years and more than $10,000 after about 30 years.

That does not mean your real lifestyle doubled. It means dollars lost purchasing power. A retirement calculation should either inflate future spending and Social Security estimates, or use real after-inflation returns and keep numbers in today’s dollars. Mixing nominal returns with today’s spending can make a plan look much stronger than it is.

Practical check: if your projection uses a 7% expected return and keeps retirement spending flat in today’s dollars, it may be too optimistic unless the return is explicitly a real after-inflation assumption.

How Social Security fits into the gap

Social Security can reduce the amount your portfolio needs to generate, but it should not be guessed casually. Your benefit depends on covered earnings, claiming age and Social Security rules. The Social Security Administration provides calculators and benefit estimates that are more useful than generic averages, and some SSA tools can show estimates in today’s dollars or inflated future dollars.

For 2026, the Social Security taxable maximum is $184,500. That figure matters for payroll taxes and the earnings base, but it does not by itself tell you your future benefit. A high earner, a mid-career worker with gaps, and a self-employed person can have very different projected benefits.

When estimating your retirement income gap, use the benefit amount that matches your assumed claiming age. Claiming at 62, full retirement age or 70 can produce materially different monthly income. This article is not a recommendation about when to claim. It is a warning that the claiming age assumption can change the entire retirement gap calculation.

Estimate portfolio income with a withdrawal rate

After Social Security, the remaining gap usually needs to be covered by retirement savings. A withdrawal rate converts a portfolio into an annual income estimate. At a 4% withdrawal rate, a $1,000,000 portfolio produces about $40,000 per year before taxes. At 3.5%, it produces about $35,000. At 5%, it produces $50,000 but with more risk.

The 4% rule is a planning shortcut, not a guarantee. It depends on market returns, inflation, retirement length, taxes, fees and spending flexibility. A person retiring at 67 with flexible travel spending faces a different risk than someone retiring at 55 with fixed high expenses. The longer the retirement and the less flexible the spending, the more conservative the withdrawal assumption should be.

Use the withdrawal rate as a stress test. If the plan works at 4.5% but fails badly at 3.5%, the margin of safety may be thin. If it works across a range of assumptions, the plan is more resilient.

Project your savings path

Once you know the approximate portfolio target, compare it with your projected savings. This is where age, current balance and monthly contributions matter. A 35-year-old with $60,000 saved and 32 years before retirement has time for compounding. A 58-year-old with the same balance has far less time and may need a different strategy.

A projection should include current retirement savings, employee monthly contributions, employer match, expected return and years until retirement. It should also respect contribution limits. For 2026, the IRS announced a $24,500 employee elective deferral limit for many 401(k), 403(b), governmental 457 and Thrift Savings Plan accounts, and a $7,500 IRA contribution limit. Catch-up rules can allow additional contributions for eligible older savers, but limits and eligibility should be checked directly.

Do not count employer match unless you understand vesting rules. A match can close part of the gap, but only if you qualify for it and keep it. Also avoid assuming that every extra dollar can go into the same account. Tax-advantaged accounts have rules; taxable savings may also be part of a retirement plan.

Example: are you on track?

Consider a 45-year-old worker who wants to retire at 67. They have $250,000 saved, contribute $900 per month, receive a $250 monthly employer match and expect a 5% real annual return. They want $5,500 per month in today’s dollars and estimate Social Security at $2,300 per month in today’s dollars.

The monthly gap is $3,200, or $38,400 per year. At a 4% withdrawal rate, the portfolio target is about $960,000 in today’s dollars. If their projected savings at 67 are above that target, they may be broadly on track. If the projection lands around $750,000, the estimated gap is not catastrophic, but it is real. They could test higher monthly savings, a later retirement age, a slightly lower spending target or a different claiming-age assumption.

The key point is that “on track” is not a feeling. It is a comparison between projected income and projected spending. The earlier the gap appears, the easier it is to address.

What to do if you have a projected shortfall

A shortfall does not automatically mean failure. It means the current assumptions do not line up. There are several levers:

  • Increase contributions. Even a moderate monthly increase can matter if retirement is years away.
  • Capture employer match. Missing a match is often one of the easiest problems to fix.
  • Delay retirement. More working years can add contributions, reduce withdrawal years and may increase Social Security.
  • Lower target spending. Housing, debt and healthcare assumptions can change the required portfolio dramatically.
  • Use a more accurate Social Security estimate. A default number may overstate or understate the actual benefit.
  • Review taxes. Traditional, Roth and taxable accounts produce different after-tax income.

The worst response is to ignore the number. The second worst response is to “solve” it by using unrealistic return assumptions. A plan that only works with very high returns is not really fixed; it is just more fragile.

Common signs your retirement plan is too optimistic

  • You use a high return but no inflation adjustment. That makes future spending look too low.
  • You assume Social Security without checking SSA.gov. Generic estimates can be wrong for your earnings record.
  • You ignore taxes. Gross portfolio withdrawals are not the same as spendable income.
  • You assume a fixed 4% withdrawal forever. Real retirement spending is affected by markets, inflation and healthcare costs.
  • You do not include healthcare or long-term care risk. Medicare does not make all medical costs disappear.
  • You assume debt is gone but have no payoff plan. Mortgage, loan and credit-card payments can dominate a retirement budget.

A practical retirement gap checklist

  1. Estimate monthly retirement spending in today’s dollars.
  2. Get a Social Security estimate from SSA.gov or an SSA calculator.
  3. Choose whether you are working in today’s dollars or future dollars.
  4. Calculate the monthly income gap after Social Security.
  5. Convert the gap into a rough portfolio target using several withdrawal rates.
  6. Project current savings and future contributions to retirement age.
  7. Compare projected savings with the target.
  8. Stress-test inflation, returns, spending and retirement age.
  9. Repeat the process at least yearly or after major life changes.
