How Much Should I Save for Retirement in 2026?

A practical guide to estimating retirement savings using spending needs, Social Security, inflation, 401(k) and IRA limits, investment return assumptions and your retirement income gap.

Short answer
There is no single retirement savings number that works for everyone. A useful 2026 estimate starts with your target monthly spending in retirement, subtracts expected Social Security, then calculates how much savings you need to fill the remaining gap. Your age, savings rate, employer match, investment return, inflation and withdrawal rate can change the answer dramatically.

Start with the income you want, not a random savings target

The common question is “How much should I save for retirement?” The better question is: “How much monthly income will I need, and how much of that income must come from my own savings?” A $1,000,000 portfolio can be more than enough for one household and not enough for another. The difference is spending, housing costs, health costs, debt, taxes, location and the role of Social Security.

A practical retirement estimate has four parts. First, estimate what you want to spend each month in retirement in today’s dollars. Second, adjust that number for inflation if you are projecting future dollars. Third, subtract expected guaranteed or semi-guaranteed income, especially Social Security. Fourth, calculate how large your portfolio needs to be to support the remaining gap.

For example, suppose you want $5,000 per month in today’s dollars at retirement and expect $2,200 per month from Social Security. That leaves a $2,800 monthly gap before taxes. If you use a 4% withdrawal assumption, that gap implies roughly $840,000 of retirement savings in today’s dollars: $2,800 × 12 ÷ 0.04. That is not a guarantee. It is a starting point for planning.

The basic retirement savings formula

A clean retirement estimate can be built with this simplified formula:

StepWhat to estimateWhy it matters
1Target retirement spendingThis is the lifestyle you are trying to fund.
2Expected Social SecurityThis reduces how much your portfolio needs to produce.
3Income gapTarget spending minus Social Security and other income.
4Withdrawal rateTurns the annual gap into a portfolio target.
5Current savings and contributionsShows whether you are likely to reach the target.

Here is the rough math. If your retirement income gap is $30,000 per year and you assume a 4% withdrawal rate, the portfolio target is $750,000. If the gap is $60,000 per year, the target becomes $1,500,000. The formula is simple, but the inputs are not. Inflation, taxes, medical costs and market returns can move the target by hundreds of thousands of dollars.

That is why a calculator is useful. It lets you change one assumption at a time. You can test whether retiring at 65 instead of 67 creates a shortfall, whether saving $300 more per month closes the gap, or whether a lower withdrawal rate makes your plan more conservative.

How much should you save each month?

Monthly savings depends mainly on how many years you have left. A 30-year-old and a 55-year-old can have the same retirement target but need very different monthly contributions. Time is powerful because contributions have longer to compound. Starting late does not make retirement impossible, but it usually requires a higher savings rate, a later retirement age, lower spending, or some combination of the three.

A useful way to think about monthly savings is to compare three numbers: your current retirement balance, your annual contributions and your target portfolio. If your current savings are small but you have 30 years left, consistent monthly contributions may still do most of the work. If retirement is 10 years away, current savings and contribution limits become much more important.

Employer match should be included, but do not treat it as free money without reading the plan rules. Vesting schedules, eligibility rules and contribution formulas matter. A $300 monthly employer match is worth $3,600 per year, but only if you stay long enough to keep it and contribute enough to receive it.

Practical rule: use a retirement calculator to test your current savings rate first. Then increase your monthly contribution until the projected gap is close to zero under conservative assumptions.

2026 contribution limits to know

For 2026, the IRS announced that the employee elective deferral limit for many 401(k), 403(b), governmental 457 and Thrift Savings Plan accounts increased to $24,500. The IRA contribution limit increased to $7,500. The IRA catch-up limit for people age 50 and older is $1,100 for 2026. 401(k)-type catch-up rules are separate, and workers in specific age bands may have higher catch-up options under SECURE 2.0 rules.

These limits do not mean everyone should automatically max out every account. They are planning ceilings. A household also needs emergency savings, debt management, insurance and enough cash flow to live normally. But the limits matter because they cap how much can go into tax-advantaged accounts each year.

Do not guess your Social Security benefit

Social Security can be a major part of retirement income, but generic estimates are weak. Your benefit depends on lifetime covered earnings, claiming age and program rules. For a better estimate, use your official Social Security account or the SSA calculators. The SSA offers benefit calculators that estimate retirement benefits and can show results in today’s dollars or future inflated dollars.

For planning, consistency matters. If your spending target is in today’s dollars, use a Social Security estimate in today’s dollars. If your calculator inflates future spending, use a future-dollar Social Security assumption or allow the calculator to inflate the number consistently. Mixing today’s dollars and future dollars is one of the fastest ways to fool yourself.

Also remember that claiming age matters. Claiming early can reduce the monthly benefit, while delaying can increase it up to the applicable maximum age. That does not mean everyone should delay. Health, employment, spouse benefits, cash needs and longevity expectations all matter. The point is simpler: do not build your retirement plan around a random Social Security number.

Inflation changes the target

If you are 35 and want to retire at 67, today’s $5,000 monthly spending target will not feel like $5,000 in 32 years. At 2.5% annual inflation, prices roughly double over that period. A plan that ignores inflation may look comfortable today but become underfunded in retirement.

There are two clean ways to handle inflation. One is a nominal projection: grow your portfolio with a nominal return and inflate your future spending target. The other is a real-return projection: subtract inflation from expected return and keep spending in today’s dollars. Both approaches can work. The mistake is mixing them.

For example, a 7% nominal return with 2.5% inflation is not the same as a 7% real return. The rough real return is closer to 4.4% using the standard adjustment. That difference compounds over decades.

Why the 4% rule is only a starting point

The 4% rule is often used because it turns a portfolio into a rough income estimate. A $1,000,000 portfolio at 4% produces about $40,000 per year before taxes. It is easy to understand and useful for quick planning. But it is not a promise.

Your safe withdrawal rate depends on retirement length, market returns, inflation, fees, taxes, asset allocation and flexibility. A retiree who can reduce spending in bad markets has more flexibility than someone with fixed high expenses. A 45-year retirement needs more caution than a 20-year retirement. A portfolio invested too conservatively can struggle to keep up with inflation; a portfolio invested too aggressively can suffer large drawdowns at the wrong time.

Use the withdrawal rate as a sensitivity test. Try 3.5%, 4% and 4.5%. If your plan only works at the most optimistic setting, it is probably fragile. If it works under conservative assumptions, you have more room for error.

Example: estimating a retirement savings target

Imagine a 40-year-old household with $120,000 already saved, $900 per month in employee contributions, $300 per month in employer match and a target retirement age of 67. They want $5,500 per month in today’s dollars and expect $2,500 per month from Social Security. Their income gap is $3,000 per month in today’s dollars, or $36,000 per year.

Using a 4% withdrawal assumption, the target portfolio needed for that gap is about $900,000 in today’s dollars. If their current savings and contributions are projected to reach or exceed that amount under a real-return assumption, they may be broadly on track. If the projection lands at $650,000, they need to adjust: save more, delay retirement, reduce target spending, improve employer match, or accept a higher risk assumption.

This is not a pass/fail judgment. It is a planning signal. The useful question is not “Am I doomed?” It is “Which lever changes the outcome most?” Often, a modest increase in monthly contributions made early can matter more than a drastic change made late.

Common retirement savings mistakes

  • Using salary instead of spending. Retirement is funded by expenses, not by your old salary. If you spend less than you earn, your required retirement income may be lower than salary replacement rules suggest.
  • Ignoring taxes. Traditional 401(k), Roth IRA and taxable brokerage accounts do not produce the same after-tax income.
  • Forgetting healthcare. Medicare does not make healthcare free. Premiums, deductibles, dental, vision, long-term care and out-of-pocket costs can be significant.
  • Assuming perfect market returns. Average return and actual sequence of returns are not the same thing.
  • Not updating the plan. A retirement estimate should be reviewed after major income changes, family changes, market shocks or tax law changes.

A practical 2026 retirement savings checklist

  1. Estimate target monthly retirement spending in today’s dollars.
  2. Get your Social Security estimate from SSA.gov or an SSA calculator.
  3. List current retirement savings by account type.
  4. Add employee contributions and employer match separately.
  5. Check 2026 IRS contribution limits before assuming you can contribute more to tax-advantaged accounts.
  6. Run both nominal and real-return projections.
  7. Stress-test the withdrawal rate.
  8. Review the gap yearly.

The goal is not to predict the future perfectly. The goal is to make the gap visible early enough that you can still change it.
