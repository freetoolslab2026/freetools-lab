How to Calculate Take-Home Pay from Salary in 2026

Understand how gross salary turns into real paycheck money after federal income tax, FICA, state tax, pay frequency and deductions.

Short answer
To calculate take-home pay from salary in 2026, start with your gross annual salary, subtract pre-tax deductions, estimate federal income tax using your filing status and standard deduction, subtract FICA taxes, add any state income tax estimate, then divide the remaining annual net pay by your number of paychecks. A salary calculator helps because each paycheck can change with benefits, deductions and state rules.

Gross salary is the number employers put in job offers. Take-home pay is the number that actually reaches your bank account. The gap between those two numbers is not a mistake: it is the result of federal income tax withholding, Social Security tax, Medicare tax, state tax in many states, pre-tax benefits and post-tax deductions.

That difference matters when you compare job offers, plan rent, budget debt payments or decide whether a raise is enough to change your monthly cash flow. A $75,000 salary does not mean $6,250 lands in your account every month. The real paycheck depends on where you live, how often you are paid and what comes out before and after taxes.

This guide explains the practical calculation step by step. It is written for W-2 employees in the United States and uses 2026 tax concepts. It does not replace payroll software, Form W-4 instructions or a tax professional, but it gives you a clear way to understand the moving parts before using a calculator.

Start with gross annual salary

Your gross annual salary is the amount you earn before taxes and deductions. If your job offer says $85,000 per year, that is the starting point. It is not the same as taxable income and it is not the same as take-home pay.

For salaried workers, gross pay is usually divided into equal paychecks. A biweekly employee gets 26 paychecks per year, a semi-monthly employee gets 24, a monthly employee gets 12 and a weekly employee gets 52. The pay frequency changes the size of each paycheck, but not the annual salary.

For example, an $85,000 salary equals about $3,269.23 per biweekly gross paycheck before deductions. The same salary equals about $7,083.33 per monthly gross paycheck. Those are gross amounts; payroll deductions still come out.

Subtract pre-tax deductions

Pre-tax deductions reduce the income used for certain tax calculations. Common examples include traditional 401(k) contributions, many health insurance premiums, HSA contributions, FSA contributions and some commuter benefits. These deductions are important because they can reduce federal taxable income.

Not every deduction affects every tax in the same way. A traditional 401(k) contribution usually reduces federal income tax wages, but it generally does not reduce Social Security or Medicare wages. Health insurance premiums under a cafeteria plan may reduce both income tax and FICA wages. A simple calculator must make assumptions, so the result is still an estimate.

The practical takeaway is simple: if you contribute more to pre-tax benefits, your take-home pay falls by less than the full contribution because some taxes may also fall. This is why a $200 pre-tax deduction may reduce a paycheck by less than $200.

Estimate federal income tax

Federal income tax is progressive. That means different slices of taxable income are taxed at different rates. You do not pay your highest marginal tax rate on every dollar. You pay each bracket rate only on the income inside that bracket.

For 2026, the standard deduction is $16,100 for single filers, $32,200 for married couples filing jointly and $24,150 for heads of household. The federal tax brackets then apply to taxable income after deductions. A salary calculator usually estimates annual federal tax first, then divides it across pay periods.

Payroll withholding is not always identical to final tax liability. Your employer withholds based on Form W-4, filing status, pay frequency, dependents, multiple jobs and additional withholding. Your final tax return can still produce a refund or balance due if withholding was too high or too low.

Subtract FICA taxes

FICA is separate from federal income tax. It includes Social Security tax and Medicare tax. For employees, Social Security tax is 6.2% on wages up to the annual Social Security wage base. In 2026, that taxable maximum is $184,500. Medicare tax is 1.45% on wages, with no general wage cap.

High earners may also owe Additional Medicare Tax. The extra rate is 0.9% above the applicable threshold: $200,000 for single filers and heads of household, and $250,000 for married filing jointly. Employers must withhold the Additional Medicare Tax once wages exceed $200,000, but final liability depends on filing status.

FICA often surprises people because it applies even when federal income tax is reduced by deductions or credits. It is one reason the first paycheck from a new job can feel lower than expected.

Add state income tax

State income tax is where salary estimates get messy. Some states have no wage income tax. Others have flat rates. Others use progressive brackets, credits, exemptions, local taxes or special payroll rules. This is why a state-by-state salary calculator should treat state tax as an estimate unless it models each state in full detail.

States with no broad wage income tax can produce a noticeably higher paycheck than states with higher income tax, but that does not mean the total cost of living is lower. Property tax, sales tax, insurance, rent, wages and housing costs also matter. A paycheck calculator is useful for cash flow, not for judging a whole relocation decision by itself.

For practical budgeting, use an editable state effective tax rate. Then compare the calculator estimate with your real payroll withholding once you have a paystub.

Account for post-tax deductions

Post-tax deductions come out after taxable wages have already been calculated. Examples may include Roth 401(k) contributions, wage garnishments, after-tax insurance, union dues or other payroll deductions. They do not reduce federal taxable income in the same way as traditional pre-tax deductions.

This distinction matters when comparing a traditional 401(k) contribution with a Roth 401(k) contribution. A traditional contribution can reduce taxable income today. A Roth contribution usually does not reduce current taxable income, so the paycheck impact is larger for the same contribution amount.

Divide by pay periods

Once you estimate annual net pay, divide by the number of pay periods. This gives your expected take-home pay per paycheck. For example, if annual net pay is $63,000, the estimated take-home amount is $2,423.08 biweekly, $2,625 semi-monthly, $5,250 monthly or $1,211.54 weekly.

Biweekly and semi-monthly pay are commonly confused. Biweekly means every two weeks, usually 26 paychecks per year. Semi-monthly means twice per month, usually 24 paychecks per year. A semi-monthly paycheck is larger because there are fewer checks, even if annual pay is the same.

Example: $85,000 salary

Suppose a single employee earns $85,000 per year, contributes $6,500 pre-tax, has no post-tax deductions, lives in a state with an estimated 5% effective state tax rate and is paid biweekly. The calculation starts with salary, subtracts the pre-tax deduction, estimates taxable income after the standard deduction, calculates federal income tax, subtracts Social Security and Medicare, then subtracts state tax.

The exact answer depends on payroll assumptions, but the structure is what matters. Federal tax is not simply salary multiplied by one flat percentage. FICA has its own rules. State tax may be zero, flat or progressive. Benefits can change taxable wages. Pay frequency changes the paycheck amount but not the annual net.

That is why the best estimate usually comes from entering your own salary, state, filing status, pay periods and deductions into a calculator rather than relying on a generic percentage.

Why your actual paycheck can differ

Even a careful estimate may not match payroll exactly. Your Form W-4 can add or reduce withholding. Your employer may treat some benefits differently. Local taxes may apply. Some states have disability insurance, paid family leave or local payroll taxes. Bonuses and supplemental wages can be withheld differently from regular salary.

Another common reason is timing. If you start a job mid-year, receive a raise, change benefits, adjust retirement contributions or get a bonus, payroll may not behave like a simple annualized estimate. A calculator gives a planning estimate; your paystub is the source of truth for what was actually withheld.

Important: If you need an exact payroll result, compare the estimate with your actual paystub and Form W-4. For tax filing decisions, use IRS guidance or a qualified tax professional.

How to use a salary calculator well

Do not just enter your gross salary and stop. The quality of the result depends on the quality of the inputs. Enter your filing status, state, pay frequency and any recurring deductions. If you know your actual state withholding rate or dollar amount from a paystub, use that instead of a generic state estimate.

For job offers, run several versions. Compare the new offer with your current job, including health premiums, retirement match, pay frequency and state. A higher salary can still feel disappointing if benefits are more expensive or state tax is higher. On the other hand, better pre-tax benefits can improve net cash flow even if gross pay is similar.

For budgeting, focus on conservative estimates. It is safer to plan rent, savings and debt payments from a slightly lower paycheck estimate than to assume a best-case number and run short.

Bottom line

Take-home pay is not just salary divided by 12 or 26. It is the result of federal tax, FICA, state tax, pay frequency, pre-tax deductions and post-tax deductions. Understanding those pieces helps you compare offers, negotiate salary, plan a budget and avoid surprises on payday.

The fastest practical approach is to estimate annual net pay first and then divide by your pay periods. Use official tax rules for federal and FICA, treat state tax as editable unless you have exact payroll data, and update the calculation whenever your benefits, salary or filing situation changes.
