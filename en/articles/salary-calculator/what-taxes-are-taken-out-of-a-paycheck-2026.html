What Taxes Are Taken Out of a Paycheck in 2026?

Understand the main paycheck taxes in the United States: federal income tax, Social Security, Medicare, Additional Medicare Tax and state income tax.

Short answer
The main taxes taken out of a paycheck in 2026 are federal income tax withholding, Social Security tax, Medicare tax, and, in many states, state income tax. High earners may also see Additional Medicare Tax. Some workers also have local income taxes. Benefits and retirement contributions are deductions, not taxes, but they still reduce the amount deposited into your bank account.

A paycheck is usually smaller than people expect because several different items are removed before money reaches the employee. Some of those items are taxes. Others are payroll deductions for benefits, retirement savings, insurance premiums, flexible spending accounts or other employer programs. Understanding the difference matters because taxes follow government rules, while deductions often depend on choices you made during onboarding or open enrollment.

For most W-2 employees in the United States, the tax side of a paycheck has four big pieces: federal income tax, Social Security tax, Medicare tax and state income tax. Federal income tax is based on taxable income, filing status and withholding information. Social Security and Medicare are payroll taxes under FICA. State tax depends heavily on where you live or work. A few states have no wage income tax, while others have flat rates, brackets, credits, local add-ons or payroll-specific rules.

This guide explains what usually comes out of a paycheck in 2026, why each item exists and why your withholding may not match a simple percentage of your salary. It is written for employees, not self-employed workers. Contractors, business owners and freelancers have different tax mechanics, including estimated tax payments and self-employment tax.

Federal income tax withholding

Federal income tax withholding is usually the largest tax line on a paycheck. It is an advance payment toward your federal income tax bill for the year. Your employer withholds it from each paycheck and sends it to the IRS. When you file your tax return, the IRS compares your total tax liability with the amount already withheld. If too much was withheld, you may receive a refund. If too little was withheld, you may owe more.

Federal income tax is not a flat percentage of gross salary. It is based on taxable income after deductions and is calculated using progressive tax brackets. In 2026, the federal standard deduction is $16,100 for single filers, $32,200 for married couples filing jointly and $24,150 for heads of household. That standard deduction reduces taxable income before brackets apply.

Progressive brackets mean that different slices of income are taxed at different rates. If part of your income falls in the 22% bracket, that does not mean your entire salary is taxed at 22%. Lower slices are taxed at lower rates first. This is why marginal tax rate and effective tax rate are different. Your marginal rate is the rate on the next dollar of taxable income. Your effective rate is the average tax rate across your income.

Payroll withholding does not calculate your final tax return perfectly. It uses Form W-4 settings, filing status, dependents, extra withholding and payroll assumptions. If you have multiple jobs, a working spouse, bonuses, stock compensation, side income or large credits, your paycheck withholding can be too high or too low. That is why two people with the same salary can have different federal withholding.

Social Security tax

Social Security tax is part of FICA. For employees, the Social Security tax rate is 6.2% of wages up to the annual wage base. In 2026, the Social Security wage base is $184,500. Wages above that amount are not subject to the employee Social Security tax for that year.

This creates a simple pattern for many workers. If your wages are below the wage base, Social Security tax is generally 6.2% of wages. If your wages exceed the wage base, Social Security tax stops after the maximum taxable amount has been reached. For 2026, an employee earning at or above the $184,500 wage base would pay $11,439 in Social Security tax for the year before considering any unusual payroll issues.

Because Social Security tax has a wage base, high earners may notice their paycheck increases later in the year once the wage base has been reached. That does not mean federal income tax or Medicare tax stopped. It means the Social Security portion of FICA has hit its annual limit.

If you change jobs during the year, payroll can get more complicated. Each employer may withhold Social Security tax based on the wages it pays you. If too much Social Security tax is withheld across multiple employers, the excess is generally handled on your tax return rather than directly by one employer.

Medicare tax

Medicare tax is the other main part of FICA. For employees, the regular Medicare tax rate is 1.45% of wages. Unlike Social Security tax, there is no annual wage base for regular Medicare tax. That means all covered wages are subject to Medicare tax, even above the Social Security wage base.

This is one reason high earners still see FICA withholding even after Social Security tax stops. The Social Security piece may end at the wage base, but Medicare continues on every dollar of wages.

Medicare tax is generally easier to understand than federal income tax because it is not based on deductions, filing status or tax brackets. It is a payroll tax applied to wages. However, high earners may also be subject to Additional Medicare Tax, which is a separate 0.9% tax above certain thresholds.

Additional Medicare Tax

Additional Medicare Tax applies to Medicare wages above threshold amounts. The IRS threshold is $200,000 for single filers, heads of household and several other statuses, and $250,000 for married filing jointly. Payroll withholding has a practical rule: employers must withhold Additional Medicare Tax once wages paid by that employer exceed $200,000 in a calendar year, regardless of the employee's filing status.

This can create mismatch. A married worker may have Additional Medicare Tax withheld by an employer after wages exceed $200,000, even though the joint tax threshold is $250,000. Another household may owe Additional Medicare Tax at filing because combined income crosses the married-filing-jointly threshold, even if neither employer withheld enough during the year.

For most workers, Additional Medicare Tax never appears. But for higher salaries, bonuses or combined household income, it is worth understanding because it can affect both paycheck withholding and final tax return results.

State income tax

State income tax is where paycheck estimates get messy. Some states have no broad wage income tax. Others use flat rates. Others use progressive brackets, deductions, exemptions, credits and local rules. Some states also have city or county income taxes. A salary calculator can estimate state tax, but exact payroll withholding often requires state-specific forms and employer payroll logic.

For example, a worker in Texas or Florida may not see a state wage income tax line, while a worker in California, New York, Oregon or Minnesota may see a significant state withholding amount. The same gross salary can produce different take-home pay depending on the state.

State tax is also not always the same as final state tax liability. Payroll withholding is an estimate. If you receive bonuses, move states, work remotely, change jobs or have non-wage income, your state tax return may differ from what was withheld. Local wage taxes can further complicate the picture in some cities and municipalities.

For planning, it is reasonable to use an editable state effective rate if you do not have exact payroll data. Once you receive a real paycheck, you can replace the estimate with actual state withholding to make future calculations more accurate.

Local payroll taxes

Some employees also pay local income tax, occupational tax, city wage tax, school district tax or other location-specific payroll taxes. These are not universal, and they are one reason national salary calculators cannot perfectly match every paycheck.

Local taxes depend on where you live, where you work and how the local rule is written. They may apply only in certain cities or school districts. They may be withheld by employers or paid separately. Because local taxes are so specific, a general calculator usually treats them as outside the main estimate unless it has local payroll data.

If your paycheck includes a local tax line, do not ignore it when budgeting. It can make a noticeable difference, especially in places with city wage taxes. The safest approach is to use your paystub as the source of truth after your first paycheck.

Pre-tax deductions are not taxes, but they reduce taxable pay

Many paycheck reductions are not taxes. Health insurance premiums, traditional 401(k) contributions, HSA contributions, FSA contributions and commuter benefits may be pre-tax deductions. They reduce the cash in your paycheck, but they may also reduce taxable wages for federal income tax and sometimes for FICA or state tax depending on the deduction type.

This is why a pre-tax deduction does not reduce take-home pay dollar for dollar in every case. If you contribute $200 to a traditional 401(k), your paycheck may fall by less than $200 because federal taxable income falls too. But the money is still leaving your immediate paycheck, so it matters for cash flow.

Pre-tax deductions are important when comparing job offers. A job with higher health premiums may produce lower take-home pay than expected. A job with a strong employer match may make retirement savings easier even if the cash paycheck looks smaller. The paycheck alone does not show the full value of benefits, but it does show immediate spendable income.

Post-tax deductions reduce take-home pay after taxes

Post-tax deductions come out after taxes are calculated. Examples may include Roth 401(k) contributions, some insurance products, wage garnishments, union dues, charitable payroll giving or after-tax benefit deductions. Because they are post-tax, they usually do not reduce federal taxable income in the same way pre-tax deductions do.

A Roth 401(k) contribution is a common example. It can be a valuable retirement strategy, but it does not reduce current federal taxable income like a traditional 401(k) contribution. That means a Roth contribution can reduce your paycheck more than a pre-tax contribution of the same amount, although the long-term tax treatment is different.

For paycheck planning, separate pre-tax and post-tax deductions. Combining them into one line can make your estimate wrong because they interact with taxes differently.

Why bonuses can have different withholding

Many employees notice that bonuses, commissions or supplemental wages have different withholding than regular paychecks. That does not necessarily mean the bonus is taxed at a special final rate. It often means payroll is using a supplemental withholding method.

Supplemental withholding can make a bonus paycheck look heavily taxed, especially after federal withholding, FICA, state tax and benefit deductions. But the final tax owed is reconciled on the annual tax return. If too much was withheld, it may increase your refund. If too little was withheld, you may owe more.

Bonuses can also push wages above the Social Security wage base or Additional Medicare Tax threshold. That can change what appears on later paychecks. For high-income employees, this is one reason a paycheck calculator is an estimate rather than an exact payroll replica.

Example: what comes out of an $85,000 salary

Suppose a single employee earns $85,000 in 2026, contributes $6,500 pre-tax, has no post-tax deductions and is paid biweekly. The paycheck estimate starts with gross salary, subtracts pre-tax deductions, applies the standard deduction and federal brackets, calculates Social Security and Medicare, then estimates state tax.

The federal taxable income will not be $85,000. It is reduced by pre-tax deductions and the standard deduction. Social Security tax, however, is generally based on covered wages up to the wage base. Medicare applies to wages without the Social Security wage cap. State tax may follow different rules depending on the state.

The final take-home pay is the amount left after all those pieces. The exact paycheck may differ because payroll systems, W-4 elections, state rules and benefits vary. But the structure is the same: gross pay minus taxes and deductions equals net pay.

How to read your paystub

A paystub is the best place to verify what is actually happening. Look for gross pay, federal income tax, Social Security, Medicare, state withholding, local withholding, pre-tax deductions, post-tax deductions and net pay. Also check year-to-date totals. Year-to-date Social Security wages are useful if you are close to the wage base.

If your paycheck seems wrong, do not guess. Compare your paystub to your benefits elections, W-4, state withholding form and salary agreement. Payroll errors happen, but many paycheck surprises come from benefit deductions, missed W-4 updates, unpaid leave, bonus withholding or state/local tax differences.

When you use a calculator, enter numbers in the same categories as your paystub. Put traditional 401(k), HSA or pre-tax health premiums under pre-tax deductions. Put Roth contributions or after-tax deductions under post-tax deductions. Use your actual pay frequency. Replace estimated state tax with the actual withholding rate if you know it.

When to update your paycheck estimate

Update your paycheck estimate whenever your salary changes, you move states, change filing status, add or remove dependents, change health insurance, increase retirement contributions or start a second job. You should also update it after a major bonus or after open enrollment if benefits premiums change.

A paycheck estimate is not something to calculate once and forget. The more variables change, the less useful an old estimate becomes. This is especially true for workers with variable compensation, high income, multiple jobs, remote work across states or significant pre-tax deductions.

Bottom line

The main taxes taken out of a paycheck in 2026 are federal income tax withholding, Social Security tax, Medicare tax and state income tax where applicable. Higher earners may also see Additional Medicare Tax. Some workers may also have local payroll taxes.

Benefits and retirement contributions are not the same as taxes, but they can reduce take-home pay just as much. To understand your paycheck, separate taxes from deductions, use your actual pay frequency and compare the estimate with your first real paystub.
