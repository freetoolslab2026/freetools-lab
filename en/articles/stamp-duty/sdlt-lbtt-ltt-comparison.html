SDLT vs LBTT vs LTT: how UK property taxes differ

Detailed comparison of England and Northern Ireland SDLT, Scotland LBTT and Wales LTT, including reliefs, surcharges and examples.

The practical difference

SDLT, LBTT and LTT are progressive taxes on property purchases, but they are not interchangeable. SDLT is administered by HMRC for England and Northern Ireland. LBTT is administered by Revenue Scotland. LTT is administered in Wales.

The calculation changes by region, buyer type and effective date. A standard home mover, first-time buyer, additional-property buyer and non-UK resident can all produce different results.

First-time buyers

In England and Northern Ireland, first-time buyer relief applies within price limits. From April 2025, qualifying buyers pay 0% up to £300,000 and 5% on the slice up to £500,000.

In Scotland, first-time buyer relief increases the LBTT nil band to £175,000, reducing tax by up to £600. Wales does not use the same first-time buyer relief structure, so a Welsh calculation should not import English rules.

Planning point

First-time buyer does not mean the same saving across the UK.

Additional properties

Additional-property treatment creates the biggest differences. England and Northern Ireland add 5 percentage points to SDLT bands. Scotland charges LBTT plus 8% ADS for transactions on or after 5 December 2024. Wales uses higher residential LTT rates.

Non-UK residents

HMRC applies a 2% SDLT surcharge for non-UK residents buying residential property in England or Northern Ireland, subject to detailed residence rules. The surcharge can apply on top of other SDLT rates.

Residence for SDLT is not simply nationality. Buyers with recent relocation, overseas work or split residence should confirm status before completion.

Thresholds and contents

Thresholds matter, especially near relief limits. Marginal bands usually tax slices, not the whole price, but relief cliffs and surcharges can still create sharp changes.

Genuine moveable contents may be separated, but inflated contents figures are risky. Carpets, curtains, furniture and appliances need realistic values and conveyancer approval.

Practical examples and checks before completion

A reliable property tax estimate starts with the correct jurisdiction, buyer status and effective date. Those three inputs matter more than a rough national average. If any of them is wrong, the final liability can be materially different. This is why a serious calculator should show assumptions clearly and should not hide the rate system behind a single black-box number.

The second check is to test scenarios. Run the calculation as a standard buyer, a first-time buyer if relevant, and an additional-property buyer where there is any doubt. If you are replacing a main residence, check the refund rules before relying on a future reclaim. A refund can improve the final position, but it does not remove the need for cash at completion.

Buyers close to thresholds should test prices slightly above and below the boundary. Marginal bands mean the whole price is not usually taxed at the higher rate, but relief limits and additional-property rules can still create sharp changes. Knowing that before negotiating can make the difference between an affordable offer and an unpleasant surprise.

Contents and fixtures need careful treatment. Genuine moveable items can sometimes be separated, but inflated or artificial values are risky. The contract should reflect economic reality. If the tax saving is based on a contents figure, keep a simple schedule showing the item, estimated value, age and condition, and let the conveyancer decide how it should be documented.

Finally, keep the calculation, the official source links and the conveyancer confirmation in one folder. Property taxes are deadline-driven. Having the figures organised makes it easier to spot changes, explain the budget to a lender and avoid confusion between tax due, legal fees and cash needed on completion.

Final checklist before relying on the estimate

First, confirm that the price entered is the chargeable consideration and not a rounded asking price. Then check region, buyer status and completion date. These inputs drive the tax calculation and can change the result much more than small legal fees.

Second, compare cash due at completion with long-term cost. A refund possibility does not remove the initial cash requirement. If a surcharge is paid first and reclaimed later, the buyer still needs funds or financing to bridge the period.

Third, keep the official links and calculation output with your conveyancing file. When rates change, written evidence of the assumptions used helps avoid confusion between an old quote, a current calculator result and the final return filed after completion.

Fourth, treat contents and reliefs carefully. The correct approach is to document genuine facts, not to create artificial values. If a saving depends on a relief, make sure the conditions are met before exchange, not after completion.

Finally, rerun the calculation after every price change. A reduction agreed late in the transaction may change the tax, while a higher offer or changed buyer status may remove relief. A serious estimate is a live part of the buying process.

Additional worked checks

Before relying on the result, run the calculation once more with a slightly higher and lower purchase price. This shows whether the result changes because of a marginal band, a relief threshold or an additional-property rule. It also helps buyers understand which assumptions are driving the final amount.

Keep a copy of the calculation with the official source links and the conveyancer’s advice. Property tax is not only a formula; it is also a filing obligation with deadlines, evidence and buyer-status conditions. A clear record reduces confusion when the transaction moves from offer to completion.

The best use of a calculator is not to replace professional advice, but to make that advice more focused. If the estimate already shows the likely tax, cash requirement and sensitive thresholds, the buyer can ask better questions and avoid last-minute surprises.

FAQ

Is LBTT the same as SDLT?

No. Scotland has separate bands, relief and ADS rules.

Does Wales use stamp duty?

Wales uses Land Transaction Tax.

Can I reclaim a surcharge?

Sometimes when replacing a main residence, but rules and deadlines matter.

Can contents reduce tax?

Only genuine moveable contents with realistic values.
